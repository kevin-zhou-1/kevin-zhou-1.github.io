Probability Distributions 101

Imagine you are going camping - it’s late and pitch black outside - so you decide to do some stargazing.

While stargazing, you can’t help but notice that sometimes a tiny speck of light would dash across the sky, only to vanish moments later. You just witnessed a shooting star.

For the purpose of this exercise, we will assume the following things about shooting stars.

  • They come and go quick, practically instantaneously.
  • Generally, you can only see one shooting star at a time.
  • There is no upper bound to the amount of shooting stars you can see at a time.
  • Shooting stars appear randomly with no predictable pattern, and there is no preferred time for them to appear (equally likely to appear during any time of the night).
  • Shooting stars appear independently of each other and seeing one shooting star does not give you any information about the arrival times (if at all) of any other shooting star.

Bernoulli Distribution

Question

You decide to watch shooting stars for an hour. What is the likelihood that you see at least one shooting star within that timeframe?

Response

We know shooting stars appear randomly. So we can have either two outcomes

  • We see at least one shooting star with some probability ppp﻿.
  • We don’t see a shooting star at all during that period with some probability 1−p{1-p}1−p﻿.

This is called a Bernoulli distribution - the most common example people give is a coin toss which yields either heads or tails (two outcomes again).

Conclusion

That’s a mouthful - but the important takeaway is that all of these distributions fundamentally describe the same natural phenomena!

We have an event that

  • has a uniform “tendency” to happen over time
  • has independent occurrences (memoryless)

This is why these distributions are so ubiquitous and generally useful.

For example, the number of particles that decay within a certain amount of time in a clump of U-235 can be modelled using a Poisson process. The waiting time between each decay event can be modelled as an exponential variable and the waiting time for xxx﻿ particles to decay can be modelled as a gamma process.

Actuaries use the Poisson process to model claim numbers for a portfolio in a year and the Gamma process to model the claim cost. The latter may sound a little counterintuitive at first - but an intuitive explanation is that we can assume claims cost accumulate at a constant rate naturally over time, it is only when they are discovered (a claim being lodged) that the full claims cost (up to that point) is revealed. The claims cost is then reset back to zero and the accumulation process begins anew.
